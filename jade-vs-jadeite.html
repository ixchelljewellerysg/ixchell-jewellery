Is Jadeite
The Same As Jade?

Technically yes. Scientifically no. And somewhere between geology, royalty and obsession, the answer becomes rather fascinating.

Jade Explained Properly

Jade Is A Family.
Jadeite Is One Member.

Most people casually say “jade”, rather like saying “car”. The problem is that jade actually refers to two entirely different minerals: jadeite and nephrite.

Jadeite is the rarer and denser version, prized for its sharper colours, finer crystal structure and luminous appearance. Nephrite is older in human history and was heavily used across ancient China, Central Asia and New Zealand long before jadeite became fashionable.

So yes, jadeite is jade. But not all jade is jadeite. A bit like saying champagne is wine, though not every wine gets invited to Buckingham Palace.

Jadeite vs Nephrite

Why Jadeite Looks More Luxurious

Scientifically speaking, jadeite is a pyroxene mineral, while nephrite belongs to the amphibole family. Yes, geology occasionally sounds like Hogwarts.

Jadeite has a tighter crystal structure, which allows light to travel through the stone differently. That is why fine jadeite often appears glowing, watery or glass-like under natural lighting.

Nephrite tends to look softer and more oily or creamy. Beautiful in its own way, certainly, but visually very different once you place both stones side by side.

This is also why collectors across Singapore, Hong Kong and Taiwan often focus on high-quality Burmese jadeite rather than generic “jade”.

Origins Around The World

Where Jadeite And Nephrite Come From

Natural jadeite is found in only a handful of places globally. The most famous source is Myanmar, formerly Burma, which produces the finest gem-quality jadeite ever discovered.

Other jadeite deposits exist in Guatemala, Japan, Russia and parts of Kazakhstan, though Burmese material remains the benchmark for top collector-grade pieces.

Nephrite, meanwhile, is more widely distributed and can be found in China, Canada, Russia, New Zealand and parts of Central Asia.

Historically speaking, ancient China originally treasured nephrite for thousands of years before Burmese jadeite quietly entered the story and changed imperial taste forever.

Type A vs Type B vs Type C

The Difference Is Treatment.
Not Marketing.

Type A jadeite means the stone is natural and untreated, aside from traditional polishing and shaping. This is the standard serious collectors usually look for.

Type B jadeite has been chemically bleached and filled with resin to improve clarity. It may initially appear bright, but long-term durability can become questionable.

Type C jadeite has been dyed to enhance colour. Under strong lighting, the colours can sometimes appear unusually loud or concentrated, rather like a nightclub attempting to become a gemstone.

A proper gemmological report will usually state “Type A” rather than “Grade A”. The wording matters more than most people realise.

Why Jadeite Changes In Different Lighting

Jadeite Is Infuriatingly Honest

One reason jadeite confuses first-time buyers is because it behaves differently under every lighting condition imaginable.

Warm lighting may make lavender tones appear richer. Natural daylight may suddenly reveal green undertones hidden indoors. Some stones look icy and translucent outdoors, then become softer and moodier inside.

This is not deception. It is simply how tightly packed mineral crystals interact with light.

Ironically, the better the jadeite, the more personality it tends to have.

Burma, Qing Dynasty & Royal Fascination

How Jadeite Entered Imperial China

For most of Chinese history, nephrite dominated imperial culture. Jadeite only entered China in larger quantities during the late Qing Dynasty through trade routes connected to Burma.

At first, jadeite was simply exotic. Then the imperial court saw its vivid emerald greens, icy translucency and lavender tones. That was the beginning of a national obsession.

One of the most famous stories involves Empress Dowager Cixi, whose fascination with Burmese jadeite helped elevate it into a symbol of status, refinement and imperial wealth.

Even today, repaired jadeite bangles associated with the Qing court remain displayed within the Palace Museum collections in Beijing — a quiet reminder that true jewellery value is not always about perfection, but legacy.
