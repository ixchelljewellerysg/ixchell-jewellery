Type A vs Type B
vs Type C Jadeite

The difference is not merely appearance. It is structure, treatment, translucency and long-term integrity. Three small letters. Surprisingly large consequences.

Quick answer: Type A jadeite is natural untreated jadeite. Type B jadeite is usually acid-bleached and polymer-filled. Type C jadeite is dyed. Type B + C jadeite has been both chemically treated and dyed.

For serious buyers, treatment status should be understood before judging colour, price or setting.

Quick Answer

What do Type A, Type B and Type C jadeite actually mean?

Type A jadeite is natural jadeite that has not been chemically treated, dyed or filled with polymer resin. Type B jadeite has been acid-washed and resin-filled. Type C jadeite has been dyed to improve or intensify colour.

In simple terms, Type A keeps its natural internal structure intact. Type B and Type C have been altered by chemical or colour treatment.

A surprising number of buyers spend more time comparing shades of green than understanding whether the stone itself remains natural. Rather like shopping for wine based entirely on bottle shape.

Natural Jadeite

Type A

Type A jadeite is natural jadeite. It may be carved, polished and shaped, but it has not been dyed, acid-bleached or resin-filled.

Its crystal structure remains naturally intact, which is one reason serious collectors and luxury buyers usually focus on Type A Burmese jadeite.

Bleached & Filled

Type B

Type B jadeite has been chemically bleached, usually using acid, to remove darker patches or impurities within the stone.

Because the bleaching process weakens the internal structure, polymer resin is then added to stabilise and improve appearance.

Dyed Jadeite

Type C

Type C jadeite has been artificially dyed to intensify or change colour. Bright greens, vivid lavender or unusually concentrated colour may sometimes indicate treatment.

The issue is not merely beauty. It is whether the colour naturally belongs to the stone in the first place.

Refractive Index 1.66

Why fine Type A jadeite appears luminous.

Fine jadeite typically has a refractive index around 1.66, which gemmologists may use as one of the indicators when identifying jadeite.

In less intimidating English, refractive index affects how light bends and travels through the mineral structure. This is part of the reason fine jadeite can appear glowing, watery or softly illuminated instead of flat or chalky.

Good jadeite does not merely reflect light from the surface. Light partially travels into the stone before returning outward gently, creating the quiet glow collectors often describe as “alive”.

The better the jadeite, the more difficult it becomes to explain using only photographs.
Translucency & Crystal Structure

Why untreated jadeite behaves differently under light.

One of the reasons fine Type A jadeite feels different in person is translucency. Natural jadeite with tightly packed crystal structure allows light to move through the stone more elegantly.

This is why some jadeite appears icy, watery or softly glowing under natural daylight, while lower-quality or heavily treated material may appear duller or unnaturally glassy.

When acid bleaching and resin filling occur, the original mineral structure becomes altered. The difference may not always be obvious in photographs, but over time it affects durability, texture and optical behaviour.

Why Treatment Matters

Why serious buyers care about treatment.

Treatment changes how jadeite ages, behaves and holds value. A natural Type A piece ages with the owner rather than against them.

Type B and Type C jadeite are not automatically unattractive. Some can look visually pleasant. The issue is disclosure, stability and whether the buyer understands exactly what they are purchasing.

A treated stone sold honestly as treated jadeite is one thing. A treated piece presented as untouched natural jadeite is where trust begins quietly leaving the room.

This is also why proper gemmological reports matter. Experienced collectors may use torches, magnification and observation, but even experts still prefer laboratory confirmation.

Myanmar & Type A Jadeite

Why Burmese jadeite became the benchmark.

Myanmar remains the most recognised source for fine gem-quality jadeite because of its exceptional colour saturation, translucency and crystal structure.

The finest Burmese jadeite became especially admired during the Qing Dynasty after imperial China encountered vivid green and highly translucent material arriving through trade routes connected to Burma.

Over time, untreated Type A Burmese jadeite became associated with prestige, refinement and dynastic collecting culture.

FAQ

Common Type A, B and C jadeite questions.

What is Type A jadeite?

Type A jadeite is natural jadeite that has not been chemically treated, dyed or polymer impregnated.

What is Type B jadeite?

Type B jadeite is usually acid-bleached and then filled with polymer resin to improve appearance and stability.

What is Type C jadeite?

Type C jadeite has been artificially dyed to intensify or change its colour.

Does Type A mean high value?

No. Type A means natural treatment status. Value still depends on colour, translucency, texture, structure, thickness, rarity and craftsmanship.
